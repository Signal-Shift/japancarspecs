## Why tyres trip people up

Japan’s domestic market runs on **metric speed ratings**, **JATMA** sizing conventions, and tyre models that may never have been sold at your local fitter. A car can arrive on **perfectly legal rubber for export** and still raise questions at **registration inspection**, **insurance survey**, or your first **MOT / NCT / state check** — because the question is not “was it fine in Japan?” but “does it meet **what my jurisdiction expects today**?”

**This article is general information, not legal or compliance advice.** Always confirm requirements with your **registration authority**, **authorised inspection station**, and insurer.

## What you should inspect before the car ships (or immediately on arrival)

Regardless of destination:

- **Age and cracking** — rubber hardens; old tyres can look fine and fail under load. Check **DOT date codes** and sidewall condition.
- **Matching axle pairs** — mismatched sizes or wildly different tread depths can fail inspections or unsettle stability control.
- **Space-saver or mismatched spare** — know what is in the well; it may be fine for Japan but not what you want on a motorway shakedown.
- **TPMS** — if your market expects **tyre pressure monitoring**, confirm sensors, compatibility, and whether the dash throws warnings after registration.

If you are buying from an auction sheet, remember: **tyre condition is a snapshot**; replacements may be needed even when the grade looks high.

## European Union / Ireland / UK-oriented view

In Europe, buyers and testers often care about:

- **Correct size and load/speed index** for what the vehicle registration documents and tyre placard allow (or what your approval paperwork permits after import).
- **Tread depth** minimums at inspection (MOT in the UK, NCT in Ireland, national equivalents elsewhere).
- **Tyre labelling and winter rules** in some countries — **M+S / 3PMSF** and seasonal requirements vary by member state for certain roads or seasons; a “summer tyre from Japan” is still a summer tyre when it lands.

Practical pattern:

1. Photograph the **tyre placard** and current tyre sidewalls before purchase.
2. Ask your import agent whether **homologation or registration** requires a specific **speed/load** pairing.
3. Budget for a **fresh set** if dates are old or sizes are odd — it is cheaper than a failed inspection or a rejected insurance condition.

## United States–oriented view

U.S. concerns often cluster around:

- **DOT markings** and whether tyres are appropriate for **highway use** under FMVSS expectations your state inspector applies (enforcement and inspection style vary by **state**).
- **Load range** on trucks and vans — Japanese placards still matter; swapping to common U.S. sizes sometimes simplifies service but must stay within safe **overall diameter** and **load** limits for the vehicle.
- **TPMS** on newer cars — clearing TPMS faults can be a registration or dealer headache if sensors are dead or incompatible.

Again: **state motor vehicle departments and safety inspection programmes differ.** What passes in one state may be questioned in another.

## Japan-specific habits that surprise buyers

- **Narrow, fuel-economy-biased tyres** on kei cars and city hatches — adequate in context, but you may want **more reserve wet grip** for your local climate.
- **Studless winter tyres** left on the car — great in Hokkaido; wrong season or illegal in your region if treated as year-round rubber.
- **Right-hand-drive** does not change tyre physics, but it does change **kerb strike** patterns — inspect inner sidewalls on the passenger side.

## Tie it back to the chassis

Tyres are wearable **consumables**; **factory specification** for allowed sizes, offsets, and speed ratings lives with the **vehicle identity** you are importing. When you order a JapanCarSpecs report, you are anchoring **trim and codes** to that identity — use that document with your fitter or compliance shop so tyre choices stay **consistent with what the car actually is**, not what the advert guessed.
