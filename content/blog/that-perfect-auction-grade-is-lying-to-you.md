## The uncomfortable truth in one sentence

A Japanese auction **overall grade** is a **house-specific condition summary from a short inspection** — not a universal score, not a warranty, and **not comparable** across every auction company the way a Euro NCAP star rating is.

If you read nothing else: **use the grade to sort your shortlist, not to replace your own due diligence.**

## Same car, different homework: why cross-house comparison breaks

Auction houses train inspectors, use their own diagrams, and calibrate what “4.5” or “5” means inside their lane. Two reputable houses can **disagree** on the same vehicle on the same week — different lighting, different inspector emphasis, or different policy on how prior paint is noted.

That means:

- **You cannot** rank cars purely by “I only buy 4.5 and above” without knowing **which house** issued the sheet.
- **You should** read **photos, comments, and meter notes** before you treat the big number as a verdict.

## The grade is not a history report

High grades correlate with **fewer obvious cosmetic hits** in the inspection window. They do **not** guarantee:

- accurate **odometer** readings (tampering and “unknown” notes still appear on good-looking sheets);
- absence of **hidden structural repair** beyond what the inspector caught;
- **mechanical** health — noises, weak compression, lazy auto boxes often live in **comments**, not the headline band.

Per our usual guidance: treat the odometer as **declared at inspection**, then cross-check with **history, prior records, and physical inspection** where you can.

## Where the real story hides: comments, diagrams, and “exceptions”

Seasoned buyers spend more time on:

- **Handwritten or printed comments** — oil leaks, engine notes, gearbox slip, AC weak, aftermarket parts;
- **Body diagram symbols** — replaced panels, rust, blend, waviness;
- **Interior wear** and **smoke smell** (often under-documented);
- **Auction disclaimers** — “as-is”, “not started”, “meter unverified”.

When **comments contradict the grade**, assume the **comments** are closer to the risk you are underwriting until you prove otherwise.

## House names matter — but the skill is portable

Whether the sheet says **USS**, **JAA**, **TAA**, **CAA**, or another venue, the workflow is the same:

1. Read **grade + diagrams + comments** as one bundle.
2. Map the car to **factory trim and equipment** using the **chassis / frame** (not the translated listing title).
3. Decide what **photos, third-party inspection, or price concession** you need before you wire money.
