## What you are looking at

Japanese wholesale auctions (USS, JAA, TAA, CAA, and others) issue a **condition sheet** for each vehicle: a mix of **standard codes**, **inspector handwriting**, and sometimes **auction-house-specific quirks**. The sheet is a **snapshot on one day** — not a warranty, not a full history, and not a substitute for mapping the car to **factory-correct** trim and equipment.

Treat it as a **strong hint** about cosmetics and obvious mechanical issues, then anchor serious buying decisions to the **chassis / frame number** and supporting documents.

## Overall grade (外装・内装など)

You will often see an **overall auction grade** (for example S, 6, 5, 4.5, 4, R, RA, ***). The scale is **not universal** across every auction house, and **two houses can grade the same car differently**. High grades usually mean less panel work, cleaner interior, and fewer obvious faults in the quick inspection window — not “as new” unless the sheet and photos agree.

Use the grade to **prioritise attention**, not to skip your own checklist.

## Exterior and interior diagrams

Small symbols on a **body diagram** mark dents, scratches, paint blend, rust, or prior repair. **A1** might be a small scratch; **U1** might be a dent — exact legend text varies by sheet layout. **W** or wave marks can indicate waviness or filler. **X** / **XX** often signal replacement panels or heavier repair (confirm in the notes).

Interior diagrams may flag burns, tears, or dash wear. **Smell (smoke, mildew)** rarely prints clearly on paper — assume you need photos or an in-person check.

## The inspector comment column (セリフ・備考)

Handwritten or printed **comments** are where the real story often lives: **engine noise**, **oil leaks**, **gearbox feel**, **AC weak**, **aftermarket parts**, **meter “unknown”**, **accident history claims**, or **“repaired / replaced”** items. Abbreviations are dense; importers often maintain glossaries.

If a comment contradicts the grade, **trust the comment** until proven otherwise.

## Odometer and documentation flags

Sheets may show **メーター交換** (meter change), **メーター不明** (unknown true mileage), or **改** (modified). Some list **走行距離不明**. These are **red-flag zones** for value and disclosure — not automatic “walk away,” but they mean you should cross-check service history, prior registrations, and physical wear.

We cover common mileage and history patterns in [Odometer and history red flags on Japanese imports](/blog/odometer-and-history-red-flags); the auction sheet is often where those warnings first appear in writing.

## Photos and “not on the sheet”

Auction photo packs can show **undercarriage rust**, **subframe**, or **engine bay** details the front-page diagram skips. Missing photos for a grade-sensitive car is itself information: assume you need more data, not less.

## What the sheet does not prove

- **Exact factory trim and options** for that chassis (packages, limited colours, equipment codes).
- **Full accident or repair history** outside what the inspector saw.
- **Compliance or registration outcome** in your country.

That is the gap a **specification report tied to your chassis** is meant to close: structured factory-relevant facts you can compare to the listing, the sheet, and the photos.

## Practical workflow before you bid

1. Read **grade + diagrams + comments** as one story, not three isolated scores.
2. Align **chassis / frame number** on the sheet with the listing and registration data.
3. Flag **meter**, **accident**, and **major mechanical** notes for extra verification.
4. If the deal depends on a rare trim or colour, **confirm against factory data**, not only the auction title line.

When the listing is moving fast, a chassis-first spec report helps you act with **documented facts** instead of forum guesses.
